In this workshop we will be collaboratively working with Twitter data in order to surface **testing situations beyond the laboratory**.
In order to do so we are **curating datasets and environments of interpretation** so that we can make sense of testing in society together.
Today I'd like to briefly raise a few questions that we might bear in mind as we work together in this workshop. # ü§î
How does the curation of Twitter data shape the varieties of testing situations that we attend to? # ü§î
As we know, we shape data and data shapes us. But we also know that we do not *determine* data and data does not *determine* us.
Datasets "render the world in accordance with different visions, values and cultures", "prioritise certain ways of knowing over others" yet are also "**multivalent and can be used in ways other than intended, by actors other than intended**." ([Gray, Gerlitz, & Bounegru, 2018](https://doi.org/10.1177/2053951718786316))
The shape of datasets may make it easier to ask some questions than others. But we can still ask questions other than those which are prioritised.
Data can be curated to accord with different "styles of reasoning" ([Hacking, 1994](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-94-017-3596-4_3)) and lines of inquiry, e.g. - real-time monitoring ‚Äì e.g. ["*what is currently trending*"](https://getdaytrends.com/) - the production of facts or factlets about society ‚Äì e.g. ["*59% of false posts remain active on Twitter*"](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/sites/default/files/2020-04/Brennen%20-%20COVID%2019%20Misinformation%20FINAL%20%283%29.pdf).
Twitter data can be used not only for "vanity metrics", i.e. the study of "how well one is doing" through "the brute counting of page views and likes as indicators of success"...
... but it may also be used in the context of "critical analytics", e.g. dominant voice, concern, commitment, positioning and alignment ([Rogers, 2018](https://ijoc.org/index.php/ijoc/article/view/6407/2248)) and "inventive methods" or "ways to introduce answerability into a problem" ([Lury & Wakeford, 2012](https://www.routledge.com/Inventive-Methods-The-Happening-of-the-Social-1st-Edition/Lury-Wakeford/p/book/9780415721103))
"**What are the methods, materials, techniques and arrangements that we curate in order to create spaces where problems can be addressed differently?**" [Noortje Marres at EASST2018](https://twitter.com/jwyg/status/1023200997668204544) # ü§î
In this workshop we are exploring how Twitter data can be used **as a starting point for the elicitation of testing situations** (in the Deweyan sense).
Thus we are **less concerned** with establishing durable claims quantifying representative samples of Twitter activity around testing (e.g. "70% of tweets about testing are negative"; "from March to April the number of tweets about testing kits doubles"). # üôà
And we are are more concerned with how Twitter data can be used to **surface a more diverse ecology of practices and conceptions of testing in society** around COVID19. # ü•∞
We are less interested in making robust claims to quantify the prominence or popularity of different kinds of digital objects that pertain to testing...
.. and more interested in how Twitter data may enact and enable the **relational figuration of testing in society** - and how we can use it to trace relations and assocations which may inform ways of thinking differently about testing.
What is involved in the curation of Twitter data? # ü§î
We can attend to how the shape of data can shape what we attend to. We can be mindful of what is involved in the creation, curation, transformation, mediation of the datasets that participate in our questioning.
The shape of the Twitter datasets we are working with is the result of a combination of: - the architecture of the Twitter platform - the activity of Twitter users - the functioning of the Twitter API - the TCAT software that we have used - the servers that run the TCAT software - the keywords for the bin/sub-bins - the data work that we have done/will do
The large COVID19 datasets that we have collected can take (12+) hours to query.
So we have created several keyword-based thematic subsets, which we'll look at in just a moment. These are not intended to be exhaustive representations of different ways of doing and thinking about testing, but rather as **starting points for the empirical (re-)specification of testing in society**.
We'd like to ask you to document how you get along with the data subsets - including transformations, decisions, questions and other relevant working notes. This is not just to produce durable and reproducible claims, but also to collectively articulate and explore the possibilities of different styles of data work and different kinds of analytical and empirical sensibilities.
To do this we'd suggest: 1. Putting all TCAT exports into an "exports" subfolder; 2. Creating a new "sheets" in Google Sheets for each new transformation; 3. Taking collective notes on your data work, observations and findings.
These data subsets are intended to serve as **threads to follow to "tease out" testing situations**. ![](images/cat.jpg)
